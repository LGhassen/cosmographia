Solar System

Our Solar System consists of the Sun, eight planets and their natural satellites, and innumerable smaller bodies ranging from Moon-sized to dust grains. Nearly all of the matter in the Solar System is in the Sun--Jupiter, the largest planet, is over twice as massive as all the other planets combined, yet it is only 1/1000th as massive as the Sun.

The planets fall into two families: smaller, terrestrial worlds with solid surfaces in the inner solar system, and enormous gas giants in the outer solar system. The main belt asteroids orbit in the zone between the inner and outer solar system; all asteroids are much smaller the smallest planet, Mercury. Beyond the orbit of the outermost planet, Neptune, lies the Kuiper Belt, which includes Pluto, Eris, and countless smaller ice worlds.

Only some of the Solar System's many interesting worlds are present in Cosmographia. The planets and largest moons are all there, but dozens of the tiny moons of Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune are omitted. We know very little about many of these objects beyond their orbits and approximate sizes.
